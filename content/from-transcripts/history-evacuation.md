# History & Evacuation

## Pre-war village life

- Tyneham is first mentioned in the Domesday Book (1086); the name derives from Old English for "goat enclosure"
- There has been a settlement here since the Iron Age (circa 800 BC); Romans were also present (circa 1st century AD)
- By the early 20th century there were around 30 multi-generational families in the parish
- In 1683 Nathaniel Bond of Lutton purchased the estate from the Williams family; the Bonds controlled it up to the evacuation
- Tyneham House (the "Great House") had an Elizabethan Purbeck stone main section with high Victorian windows along the ground floor; described by Thomas Bond as "one of the finest country houses in Purbeck"; interior rooms were ornately decorated with views over semi-tropical plants; beyond the immaculate lawns was an avenue of tall beech trees
- Residents had no electricity and, apart from the Laundry Cottages, no running water — water came from the village well and the fountain built by Reverend Bond in 1853
- The village was described as "a backwater in a backwater... practically off the map"
- Life was centred on the Bond estate: most residents were tenants and their livelihoods tied to the squire
- Occupations: farmers, shepherds, gamekeepers, fishermen, domestic servants, coastguards
- The village lost 12 men in World War One, including all three sons of Emily Taylor (of the Laundry Cottages), who then died herself in 1917 — described as dying of "broken heart syndrome"
- After WWI, fishing declined and families drifted away; the school closed in 1932 as pupil numbers fell
- By the 1930s motor vehicles and a telephone box had arrived, but running water and electricity had still not reached most of the village

## The letter — November 1943

- On 16 November 1943 (some sources say "19th November"), letters from the War Office arrived at every household — completely out of the blue, delivered by the postman
- The letter was from Southern Command, signed by a gentleman from Southern Command
- **Quotable passage from the letter (from m-smU0jdJzY):** "The date on which the military will take over this area is the 19th of December next, and all civilians must be out of the area by that date. The government appreciates that this is no small sacrifice which you are asked to make, but they are sure that you will give this further help toward winning the war with a good heart."
- Residents had exactly 28 days to comply — in the run-up to Christmas
- 225 people from 102 properties (some sources say 106 properties) across approximately 12 square miles were evacuated
- No right of appeal — only a one-line acknowledgement of the pain it would cause

## The evacuation

- Farmers were forced to hold livestock auctions in a frantic rush; Cyril Barnes had one of the first tractors in the valley and had to leave it behind
- The Bond family (squire) received some compensation; most families were given only the value of the produce in their gardens — "woefully inadequate"
- Residents were billeted across Dorset — Corfe Castle, Weymouth and other nearby towns are mentioned
- Some men were away fighting in WWII and learned of the eviction while on active service, returning to find no home to go back to
- **The evacuation date:** 19 December 1943 — six days before Christmas

## The note on the church door

The most famous moment of the evacuation. Helen Beatrice Taylor (born 1901, aged 44 in 1943), who had grown up in the Laundry Cottages, is identified in jf9lL9wsZ3Q as pinning the note as "the symbolic last resident to leave Tyneham."

**The note (exact wording):**
> "Please treat the church and houses with care. We have given up our homes where many of us have lived for generations to help win the war to keep men free. We will return one day and thank you for treating the village kindly."

- The politeness of the note is widely noted — "please," "thank you," "kindly"
- The line "We shall return one day" is described as "heartbreaking naive optimism" (2I5XZzzvovQ)
- The note is now on display inside the church

**Note: some transcripts say Helen Taylor left the note; 2I5XZzzvovQ says "history believes it was one of the village's farmers." The jf9lL9wsZ3Q account (which gives Helen Taylor's full name and age) is the most detailed and reliable-seeming.**

## D-Day training

- The real reason for the requisition — only revealed later — was to create a live-fire training area for the Allied invasion of Europe
- The village itself became a simulated forward communications zone; soldiers practiced urban warfare in the empty cottages
- Tyneham House was used as an officer's mess, filled with maps and strategy tables (2I5XZzzvovQ says this; byKvdDDwMpQ says the RAF requisitioned it for WAAFs — see People & Families section)
- The US First Infantry Division ("The Big Red One") trained here for the Normandy landings (2I5XZzzvovQ)
- On D-Day, 6 June 1944, those men stormed Omaha Beach; 2,500 US troops trained at Tyneham died that day (VVNrWYK8fm8)
- The surrounding hills were pounded by live artillery; tank tracks scarred the ancient downlands

## Post-war betrayal

- After VE Day (May 1945), residents prepared to return — and waited. The Army did not leave
- Initial excuse: the valley was littered with unexploded ordnance
- The real reason: the Cold War had begun; the ranges were too valuable to give back
- In 1948 the final betrayal: the government issued a **compulsory purchase order**, buying all the land from the Bond family
- The entire valley was purchased for **£30,000** — described as "the princely sum of £30,000" (ZvdwhpTAC4k)
- Tenants had no legal right of return; the promise had been "a gentleman's agreement, a statement of intent, not a binding contract" (2I5XZzzvovQ)
- Ralph Bond, last owner of Tyneham House, received compensation but died in 1952 still bitter at the government's handling

## The D-Day connection — context

**Quotable (VVNrWYK8fm8, historian Linda Price):** "They all got a letter... they had basically a month to get out... they expected to come back, you know, that's what's very poignant about it. They thought the war would end, they would come back, and all would be as it was."

**Quotable (VVNrWYK8fm8, historian):** "When I look at Tyneham, what I think about is how much dislocation, how much destruction, what a human cost there is even to winning a war, even to non-combatants. The level of self-sacrifice that was necessary to conduct the kind of total war that was required to defeat the Nazis — that kind of effort boggles the modern mind."

## The village today

- Tyneham remains under MOD ownership as part of the Lulworth Ranges
- The last surviving original resident (unnamed in transcripts) died in May 2025 at the age of 100; in 2024 he made one last trip to see the house where he was born (Ldl_u6MY_fg)
- The village is described variously as "the village that died for D-Day," "Britain's abandoned WWII village," and "the UK's Pripyat"
- One transcript notes a grave dated 2003 in the churchyard, and another a stone dated 2010/2015, suggesting former residents can still be buried there

## Contradictions / uncertainties

- The number of evacuated properties varies: 102 (jf9lL9wsZ3Q, ZvdwhpTAC4k, 2I5XZzzvovQ), 106 (smtiBP1OPrc, jf9lL9wsZ3Q also), and "106 properties in the area of approximately 12 square miles" (smtiBP1OPrc)
- The person who left the note: Helen Taylor (jf9lL9wsZ3Q, smtiBP1OPrc) vs. "one of the village's farmers" (2I5XZzzvovQ)
- The evacuation date: 19 December 1943 appears consistently, though one source (7RIxF46D8FU) says "19th of November"
- Tyneham House use during the war: 2I5XZzzvovQ says American/British officers' mess; byKvdDDwMpQ and jf9lL9wsZ3Q say the RAF requisitioned it for WAAFs in 1941, before the full village evacuation — likely both happened at different stages
- One transcript (bp8gutJXm0Y) incorrectly says the village is "now owned by the National Trust" — it is MOD/Army
