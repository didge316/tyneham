## Route 1: Tyneham to Worbarrow Bay (Short Walk)

**Distance:** ~1.5 miles (2.4 km) one way  
**Time:** ~30 minutes each way  
**Grade:** Easy — well-graded, flat path  
**Accessibility:** Suitable for prams and buggies (qxakRTadU-M, Jurassic Coast Trust)

The most popular walk from the village. Head south from the car park on the authorised track.

**What you'll see on the way:**
- Gate Cottages ruins (isolated building shell on the path)
- Anti-tank **Dragon's Teeth** obstacles on the approach to the beach — installed in 1940 to prevent German tank landings (Operation Sea Lion)
- Military debris/burnt-out vehicle hulks visible on the surrounding ranges

**At Worbarrow Bay:**
- Pebble and sand beach backed by dramatic chalk and limestone cliffs
- Crystal-clear water: "The sea is very clear down there" (T9KLTxZq1Gk); "a blue with a tint of white running all the way through it due to the chalk and limestone" (UJhiuu6WjWQ)
- The rocks here record an extraordinary geological story (Purbeck Monocline): dinosaur footprints have been found in the cliffs; tilted rock strata from the Alpine collision of the African and Eurasian plates ~30 million years ago (qxakRTadU-M)
- **Swimming**: The water is inviting but the beach is within the military range — some areas may have unexploded shells beneath. One transcript notes: "Although this beach looks absolutely gorgeous and you would like a dip in the water, you can't — it is military and there's unexploded shells." Check signs for swimmable sections.
- **Worbarrow Tout**: The rocky headland at the far right of the bay. Views from the top are "out of this world" (FVNds4hVZ14). A sheltered cove on the far side of the Tout is quiet even when the main beach is busy.
- Remnants of the former fishermen's settlement — one ruined cottage still visible
- **Quotable (MEkYz_QPw9w):** "Oh, what an absolutely beautiful place to live. You can understand and sympathise terribly well with the residents of Tyneham having to move from this beautiful part of the world."

**Return:** Retrace steps back to Tyneham (30 mins).

## Route 2: Tyneham Circular — Whiteway Hill and Flowers Barrow

**Distance:** ~4 miles (6.4 km)  
**Time:** ~2.5 hours  
**Grade:** Moderate–Strenuous — one steep ascent (Whiteway Hill) and a very steep descent to Worbarrow Bay  
**Notes:** Stick to marked paths (unexploded ordnance risk). Only accessible when ranges are open.

**The Route:**
1. **From Tyneham car park**, head north up the footpath alongside the churchyard (two yellow marker posts)
2. **Ascend Whiteway Hill** — a gravel track makes the climb manageable, but it's steady. Halfway up, views of Worbarrow Bay open up behind you.
3. **Reach the ridge** — views in all directions: Lulworth Castle to the northwest, Portland Bill to the southwest, Poole Harbour to the north, Worbarrow Bay to the south, Corfe Castle visible to the east, Kimmeridge Bay further east.
4. **Follow the ridge east** (~1 mile) to **Flowers Barrow** (Iron Age hill fort — see below)
5. **Steep descent** to Worbarrow Bay — "quite steep... very pleased we were going down and not up" (o51CGikzXzs). "Definitely easier going up than coming down" (t3SnIZFYdt0 — referring to Lulworth Cove approach, but sentiment applies).
6. **At the bottom**, ignore the obvious footpath gap; instead look for a small gap in the embankment with two yellow posts — this is the correct path down into the bay (FVNds4hVZ14 warning)
7. **Worbarrow Bay** — beach stop, paddle, views from Worbarrow Tout
8. **Return to Tyneham** via the flat valley track from the bay (~30 minutes)

**Viewpoints on the ridge:**
- Tank tracks from current training are visible below (ranges in use since 1917)
- Trig point at the highest point on Whiteway Hill
- "From here you can see Lulworth Castle basking in the sunshine" (FVNds4hVZ14)
- Looking down into Tyneham: "quite beautiful up here... it really is" (FVNds4hVZ14)

## Flowers Barrow Iron Age Hill Fort

Reached via the ridge, about 1 mile east of Whiteway Hill.

- Iron Age hill fort, ~2,500 years old
- Taken over by the Romans after the invasion
- **Defences**: double and triple ramparts on the east and west sides; cliffs on the south side acted as natural defence
- Over half the fort has been **lost to coastal erosion** — the eroding cliff face has exposed hut circles in the interior
- Archaeological finds (limited excavations due to coastal erosion and unexploded ordnance risk): Iron Age pottery, sling stones, bones — evidence of trade and occupation
- An information board at the fort (sometimes difficult to read in bright sun)
- **Philip Draper (original resident), from Tyneham Action Group interview**: "For archaeologists, there's the Iron Age earthwork encampment on the top of the hill which is the only coastal hill fort in England — and is as good, I think, as Maiden Castle at Dorchester which is so famous."
- **Views from Flowers Barrow**: Portland Bill and Weymouth Bay clearly visible; Lulworth Castle below; Worbarrow Bay and the descent route; the full arc of the Jurassic Coast

## Route 3: Extended Kimmeridge–Tyneham Coastal Walk

**Distance:** ~7.5 miles (12 km) one way from Kimmeridge  
**Time:** ~3.5–4 hours one way  
**Grade:** Strenuous — significant elevation gain and loss, sections of the South West Coast Path

This is a section of the **South West Coast Path** (630 miles, Poole to Minehead) running through the Lulworth Ranges. Only accessible when ranges are open.

**Route from Kimmeridge to Tyneham:**
- Start at **Kimmeridge Bay** — rock pools, fossil hunting, the **Clavel Tower** (a Tuscan-style observatory built 1830, later coastguard lookout, dismantled and rebuilt 2005–2008 due to cliff erosion)
- Walk west along the coastal path into the Lulworth Ranges (passes the **Kimmeridge oil field** — a nodding donkey pump visible, producing oil since the early 1960s)
- Pass the ruins of old military lookout posts
- **Gad Cliff** — dramatic chalk and limestone cliff (see gad-cliff section for more). Views back towards Kimmeridge: "Just looking back at the Gad there — that is so impressive." (f8lNYFJ_YCE)
- **Brandy Bay** — below the cliffs
- **Worbarrow Bay** — join the beach or ascend Worbarrow Tout for views
- Follow the valley path inland to **Tyneham village**

**Practical notes:**
- This is a one-way route — you would need two cars or to return the same way (~15 miles round trip)
- **No mobile signal** in the ranges — carry a paper map
- Water and food essential — no facilities en route
- The ranges section is only open at weekends and school holidays — check both Tyneham AND Lulworth Range opening status

## Route 4: Figure-of-Eight from Kimmeridge (~13–15 miles)

**Distance:** ~13–15 miles (the route described in bp8gutJXm0Y, described as "a figure of eight")  
**Time:** Full day  
**Grade:** Very strenuous

Described by one presenter as "12 miles covered by the time I left Tyneham, probably around 15 miles in total."

**Rough route:**
- Start above Kimmeridge
- Head inland then drop to coast at Kimmeridge Bay
- South West Coast Path west to Worbarrow Bay
- Ascend steep hill to Flowers Barrow
- Ridge walk to Whiteway Hill
- Drop down into Tyneham village (explore)
- Climb back up to coastal path
- Retrace south-west to Kimmeridge Cap (viewpoint) then head inland back to start

**Highlights:**
- Fossils at Kimmeridge rock pools
- Clavel Tower
- Flowers Barrow hill fort
- Ridge views in both directions
- Tyneham village exploration

## Route 5: Corfe Castle Purbeck Ridge Walk (nearby, not from Tyneham)

**Distance:** ~7–8 miles circular  
**Time:** ~3 hours  
**Grade:** Moderate

Described in u9ktH4yzL7Y as a day-after companion walk to the Tyneham routes:

- Start at Corfe Castle car park
- Walk up to the castle ruins
- Climb over Noel Hill and Ridgeway Hill
- Descend north to East Creech village (duck pond visible)
- Follow the Purbeck Way east and back south along the ridge
- Return to Corfe Castle

**Views**: Poole Harbour to the north; St Aldhelm's Head and the hills above Kimmeridge to the south; the Purbeck ridge stretching east toward Swanage.

## General Walking Notes for the Area

- **Red flag = no access** — always check before travelling
- The ranges cover ~7,000 acres and have been in use since 1917
- **Stay on marked paths** — unexploded ordnance is a genuine risk in off-path areas. Signs are very clear about this.
- **Terrain**: chalky downland, steep grassy slopes, some gravel tracks. Can be very slippery after rain.
- **Footwear**: walking boots essential for the circular route; trainers acceptable for the flat Worbarrow Bay path
- **Water**: carry enough — no facilities en route. "I made sure we had plenty of water because it's quite a warm day today." (FVNds4hVZ14)
- **OS map**: OS Explorer OL15 (Purbeck and South Dorset) covers the area
- **GPS postcode for satnav** (approximate): BH20 5QH — but be aware satnav may not route correctly through the ranges

## What Makes These Walks Distinctive

- The walking combines Jurassic Coast geology, Iron Age archaeology, WWII history, and the ghost village
- **Quotable (bp8gutJXm0Y):** "This walk has so much to offer — the flowers are coming out, the skylarks sing, and life is generally rather nice on the South West Coast Path."
- **Quotable (Philip Draper):** "Geologically, it is of prime importance — it's a textbook case, it's equivalent to about 2,000 feet depth of Earth all tipped up on edge. So the geologists can see the whole strata right through along this coast."
- The paradox of walking through a live military range to reach a preserved village and pristine beach is unlike anything else in England
