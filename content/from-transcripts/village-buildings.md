## The Row (Post Office Row)

A terrace of four stone cottages is the heart of the village, known as The Row or Post Office Row. Built around 1850, originally thatched and retiled in 1880. Numbered from the church end outward:

- **No. 1 The Row — The Schoolhouse**: Home of the schoolmistress (not the school itself, which is the separate building around the corner). Names of teachers on the board: Annie Fry, Laura Woodman, Malvina Pritchard, Leonora Hearn.
- **No. 2 The Row — The Labourer's Cottage**: Home of the Whitlock/Whitlock family 1933–42/43. Ernest and Evelyn Whitlock, children Ronald and Ellen. Ernest worked at Tyneham Farm. He became an unofficial barber when troops were billeted during the war. The cottage has a small baking/bread oven attached to the fireplace.
- **No. 3 The Row — The Post Office**: Home of the Driscoll family 1938–43. Post mistresses: Jane Pitman (1907), Joan Driscoll (who delivered mail at age 16), Gwendoline Driscoll (the last postmistress). Before 1929, the only telephone in the village was in the back room of the post office. Villagers had to use telegram to communicate before that. The post office doubled as a general store: "bags of flour stacked up on the floor, business was brisk."
- **No. 4 The Row — The Shepherd's Cottage**: By tradition always occupied by the shepherd. Upshore family 1925–29 — Shepherd Bill Upshore photographed with his dog Sam.

The roofs of The Row cottages have been removed by the MOD for health and safety reasons. The walls are exposed to the elements but can be safely walked through. Each cottage has an information board with photographs of the families who lived there.

## The Telephone Box

**The most photographed feature of the village.** Outside the Post Office (No. 3 The Row).

- First public telephone installed winter of 1929 — a K1 kiosk, Mark 236 (one of Britain's very first standard telephone kiosks)
- Made of concrete with a wooden door
- Before 1929, the only phone in the village was the one in the back room of the post office; before that, villagers relied on telegrams
- Inside: original A and B button pay telephone, the number "Kimmeridge 221", wartime notice reading **"I am on war work. If you must use me, be brief."**
- The kiosk is one of only 4–5 surviving K1 boxes still in situ in the UK (jf9lL9wsZ3Q says "one of only four"; Ldl_u6MY_fg says "there's actually only five of this type of telephone box left")
- The original box was severely damaged during the filming of the 1984 film *Comrades*; the replacement was installed by the film company and was fully restored with the help of an ex-GPO engineer (Ian Jolly) to look exactly as it did in 1943
- One transcript notes the restoration happened in 1983 with "incorrect K1 roof decoration," then the filming destroyed it, and the current version dates to 2012
- The box is locked; visitors can peer through the window to see the original fittings
- **Quotable local legend (VVNrWYK8fm8):** "in the middle of the village there's an old white telephone box which seems something of an oddity in this day and age and even more odd is the local story that even though it was disconnected decades ago it still rings"

## The Village School

A separate stone building from the schoolmistress's cottage (No. 1 The Row).

- Built 1856, originally with a capacity of 60 pupils
- When the Worbarrow Bay coastguard station closed in 1911, families left and pupil numbers nearly halved
- Only 9 pupils remained in 1932 when the school was closed due to economic difficulties
- The building was then used as the village hall until the 1943 evacuation
- In 1994 (some sources say 1982/1976 cleared, 1994 fully restored) the interior was recreated to show the school as it looked in the 1920s when Mrs Malvina Pritchard was schoolmistress

**What visitors see inside:**
- Rows of original wooden desks with inkwells and hard benches
- Children's names on pegs in the corridor (coat hooks, still with name plates)
- Open nature study books on desks noting local wildlife
- The blackboard with the last lesson — on the holly tree
- A cane on the teacher's desk
- A piano (hymn sheet showing "God Save the King" and "The Girl I Left Behind Me")
- Portrait of King George V above the fireplace; portrait of Queen Victoria also present
- A coal stove for heating (no electricity in the original school — oil lamps provided light)
- Children's encyclopedia, weights and measures charts
- The school never had electricity; today it is powered by solar panels on the roof
- **Quotable (2I5XZzzvovQ):** "The school room has been meticulously recreated to look exactly as it did on its last day in December 1943. The small wooden desks are in rows. Exercise books are open on the desks. The children's handwriting still visible. On pegs on the wall, the children's coats hang. And on the blackboard are the names of the last pupils and a nature lesson about the holly tree. It is a scene of such suspended intimacy that it feels like trespassing."

The school has been Grade II listed since 2020.

## The Church — St Mary's

- Foundations date to at least the 13th century (some sources say "built in 1297"; others say "late 13th century")
- Architecture: nave, chancel, north and south transepts, west porch
- Key alterations: 1744 (considerable); chancel rebuilt early 19th century; south transept added 1832; south porch moved to west side 1880
- Grade II listed building (2020); recognised as part of the Jurassic Coast UNESCO World Heritage Site
- Before D-Day training began, the Army removed the heavy oak pulpit, pews, and stained glass and transported them to safety (the stained glass windows returned)
- Despite the War Office agreeing the church would be spared damage, it was hit by shells twice by 1950
- The two church bells were relocated to St Michael and All Angels at nearby Steeple
- A Bible found in the church is inscribed: "Last to be used in church of St Mary. Restored by Royal Armoured Corps Gunnery School in 1981 in memory of the parishioners of Church Worbarrow through the generosity of the public and the Bond family"
- Now functions as a museum for village history; oak information boards on the walls with family photographs
- The original note from the church door is now on display inside
- **The church door**: "this is the actual door where the note was pinned on December the 19th 1943" (wT_BQ1g2A4Y)
- A grave from 2003 is visible in the churchyard (presumably a former resident). Another from 2010/2015 (JZXE61qyxkY). Fresh flowers seen by visitors — the churchyard is still occasionally used for burials.
- Church family names displayed in a frieze of tiles around the interior
- The village pub, **The New Inn**, is mentioned in 2I5XZzzvovQ alongside the post office and church as the centre of social life — no other detail is given

## The Village Fountain and Well

- Fountain built 1853 by Reverend Bond (variously named John Bond or Reverend Nathaniel Bond — sources differ)
- Supplied piped water from a nearby artificial reservoir — the village's second water source after the original well
- Bible quotation from John 4:13–14: *"Whosoever drinketh of this water shall thirst again, but whosoever drinketh of the water that I shall give him shall never thirst. But the water that I shall give him shall be in him a well of water springing up into everlasting life."* (with a sign now saying "Do not drink")
- Grade II listed (RBseawpx-X4 notes this along with church and school)
- Villagers came here multiple times a day to collect water — only the Laundry Cottages had running water

**Coronation tree:** Beside the fountain is a tree planted in 1911 to commemorate the coronation of King George V. By 2022, the tree was 111 years old and very large.

## The Rectory

- Built 1853 (smtiBP1OPrc says "1876 by Reverend Nathaniel Bond"; the date is contradicted between sources)
- Described as "a far more stylish property for the time — a complete contrast to the other village buildings"
- At its height contained a barn, formal garden, greenhouse, cobbled courtyard, and tennis court
- Fire of unknown origins destroyed it — 1966 (jf9lL9wsZ3Q) or 1969 (smtiBP1OPrc). Upper levels demolished for safety by the MOD
- Now heavily overgrown; visitors cannot enter
- Reverend Sharp's car was recorded outside the rectory in 1930s photographs
- The **Rectory Cottages** (separate from the main Rectory) were home to the Biles family 1910–14; Albert Biles was gardener to the Reverend

## The Laundry Cottages

- Located on the east side of the village
- The **only property in the village to have running water** (supplied by a spring)
- Site of the village laundry — all washing for Tyneham House and the Rectory was done here
- Home of the **Taylor family** 1902–1943
  - Emily Taylor lost three sons (William, Arthur, and Bert) in WWI; died 1917 aged 52, "simply worn out with work, worry and grief for her lost children"
  - Helen Taylor (born 1901) grew up here; became the last villager to leave, pinning the famous note on the church door on 19 December 1943

## The Gwyle Cottages

- Wellman family 1912–36; Grant family 1924–42; then leased to visiting army officers 1942–43
- **Arthur Grant** visited decades later and spotted himself as a child in a 1927 photograph on display (EvX1Kd0homg)
- Ruined; fenced off as unsafe. Backs of the cottages give a sense of how the gardens would have looked.

---

## The Gardener's Cottage

- Home of the Gould family
- Tom Gould served in WWI, returned and became head gardener at Tyneham House for 40 years. Wife Virtue; son John; John's wife Murial.
- John Gould was fighting in WWII when the eviction notice arrived, learning about it while on active service
- Now fenced off; not safe to enter

## Tyneham Farm

About 300 metres south-east of the village, a short walk from the car park.

- The Dairyman's House and Farmhouses are no longer standing; the stable block, yard, and granary survive in good condition, recently restored
- The Smith family ran the farm 1860–1940; Kate and Walter K. Smith 1902–1940; Sydney Churchill took over 1940 until Army closed it in 1943
- **The Great Barn**: showcases agricultural equipment. At the turn of the 20th century it served as a **village theatre** (plays and pantomimes from ~1899 to 1914, drawing audiences by horse-drawn carriage). Reinstated as a theatre/exhibition space in 2009.
- **The hay wagon**: a meticulously restored original piece, kept in the barn
- **The tractor**: a rusted 1930s/early 1940s Fordson Major tractor — "presumably in 1943 was broken down and too difficult to remove, and probably formed part of a compensation claim" (jf9lL9wsZ3Q). Cyril Barnes had one of the first tractors in the valley and had to leave it behind.
- Stables: 6 stalls for horses. Original wood "literally rotting away." Also: carriage sheds, seed barn, hen house, bull house (the "Monster Devon Bull")
- The farm equipment was partially brought in by local historians in the past 15–20 years; much is original
- A wall of military ordnance/shells collected from the area is displayed near the toilet facilities
- **Quotable (jf9lL9wsZ3Q):** "I have to say I found walking through the barns quite eerie! Unlike maybe the church and the school... walking through the farm really does make it feel like everybody just upped and left in 1943."

## Tyneham House (The Great House)

In an EOD-uncleared area; not accessible to visitors and almost entirely demolished.

- Built 1563–1583 by Henry Williams
- Henry Williams's daughter Jane inherited; she married Captain Robert Lawrence, a Royalist; he lost his wealth and lands after the Civil War
- Nathaniel Bond purchased the estate in 1683 — beginning a dynasty of over 250 years
- Thomas Bond described it as "one of the finest country houses in Purbeck"; ornate interior rooms; semi-tropical plants in the grounds; avenue of tall beech trees; immaculate lawns
- **The Elizabethan Purbeck stone main section** with high Victorian windows along the ground floor (byKvdDDwMpQ gives the clearest description — from a WAAF stationed there)
- In 1941, the RAF requisitioned it to house WAAFs (Women's Auxiliary Air Force) for the nearby radar station at Brandy Bay; the Bonds moved to Museum Cottage in the village
- After the 1943 evacuation the house rapidly deteriorated
- The MOD demolished much of the structure in 1973 for safety; only rubble remains, surrounded by woodland
- **Quotable (jf9lL9wsZ3Q):** "I think the destruction of Tyneham House is possibly the worst piece of cultural vandalism in the whole Tyneham story. The house should have been protected in the same way the church was, and maintained for future generations — and that opportunity is now lost."

## Worbarrow Bay Fishermen's Settlement

A separate hamlet at the bay, 1.5 miles from the village (30-minute walk).

- About seven fishermen's cottages plus a coastguard station
- The coastguard station employed over half of the ~50 inhabitants at its peak; closed 1911, demolished 1912; the settlement never recovered
- Ruins of **Gate Cottages** visible on the path from Tyneham
- 1940: anti-tank **Dragon's Teeth** (concrete obstacles) installed on the approach path from the beach to prevent German landing tanks (Operation Sea Lion landing point)
- A WWII lookout post (not a pillbox — armoured but without gun slits) installed around 1940, visible above the bay

## Baltington Farm / Baltington Hall

- About half a mile west of the village; in an EOD-uncleared area; not accessible
- Started as a manor house in the mid-1600s; by 1774 listed only as a farm
- Home to the **Longman family** in 1943
- An ornamental pond in front of the barn complex is the only trace of the manor-house origin
- The main structure survives in ruined form

## Contradictions / Uncertainties

- **Rectory build date**: 1853 (jf9lL9wsZ3Q, smtiBP1OPrc in one place) vs. 1876 (smtiBP1OPrc in another place). The 1853 date is more widely cited.
- **Rectory fire date**: 1966 (jf9lL9wsZ3Q) vs. 1969 (smtiBP1OPrc)
- **Tyneham House demolition**: 1967 (2I5XZzzvovQ) vs. 1973 (jf9lL9wsZ3Q, smtiBP1OPrc). 1973 is more widely cited.
- **Telephone box count**: "one of only four" (jf9lL9wsZ3Q) vs. "only five of this type" (Ldl_u6MY_fg)
- **Post mistress identity**: The "last to leave" is called Helen Taylor by most sources (who left the note); 2I5XZzzvovQ calls the last post mistress "Mrs. Maryanne Robertson" — these may refer to different people, or it may be an error
- **Church date**: "late 13th century" is most common; "Built in 1297" (6bFtVUtgNB8) is specific but may be inaccurate
- **Rectory builder**: "Reverend Nathaniel Bond" (smtiBP1OPrc) vs. "Reverend John Bond" (jf9lL9wsZ3Q, who also built the fountain); the fountain builder is likely John/Reverend Nathaniel Bond — sources agree it was "Reverend Bond"
