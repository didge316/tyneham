## The Bond Family — Squires of Tyneham

The Bond family owned and controlled the entire Tyneham estate from 1683 to the compulsory purchase in 1948 — over 250 years.

- **Nathaniel Bond of Lutton** purchased the estate from the Williams family in 1683, beginning the dynasty
- The Bonds owned everything: the land, the cottages, the farms, and in effect the jobs. Almost all residents were their tenants.
- **Thomas Bond** described Tyneham House as "one of the finest country houses in Purbeck"
- **Reverend Nathaniel Bond** built the Rectory (1853 or 1876 — sources differ) and is associated with the village fountain
- **Reverend John Bond** built the village fountain in 1853 (jf9lL9wsZ3Q) — this may be the same person as "Reverend Nathaniel Bond" or a different family member
- **Lillian Bond** (1887–1980) is mentioned in the farm display (JZXE61qyxkY)
- **Ralph Bond** — the last owner of Tyneham House. When the RAF requisitioned the house in 1941, the Bonds were forced to move to Museum Cottage in the village. Ralph received compensation when the compulsory purchase was made in 1948, but **"died in 1952 still bitter over the government's handling of the evacuation and broken promises."** (U0mULNSpG8Q)

**The Bond family home**: Tyneham House (The Great House) — built 1563–83 by Henry Williams, purchased by the Bonds 1683. Elizabethan Purbeck stone main section, high Victorian windows along the ground floor, ornately decorated rooms, views over semi-tropical plants, immaculate lawns, avenue of tall beech trees.

## The Williams Family — Original House Builders

- **Henry Williams** built Tyneham House between 1563 and 1583
- His daughter **Jane Williams** inherited the estate
- Jane married **Captain Robert Lawrence**, a staunch Royalist in the English Civil War
- Lawrence lost his wealth and lands when the King was defeated by Cromwell and the Parliamentarians
- The estate was sold to Nathaniel Bond in 1683

## Helen Beatrice Taylor — The Note on the Church Door

- Born 1901 in the Laundry Cottages; aged 44 at the time of the evacuation
- Grew up in the Laundry Cottages with her mother Emily
- As the symbolic last resident to leave Tyneham, she pinned the famous note to the church door on 19 December 1943
- The note read: *"Please treat the church and houses with care. We have given up our homes where many of us have lived for generations, to help win the war to keep men free. We will return one day and thank you for treating the village kindly."*
- Helen did not die until **1998** (UJhiuu6WjWQ — the information board notes this). She lived 55 years in exile.

**Contradiction**: 2I5XZzzvovQ says "history believes it was one of the village's farmers" who left the note, and names "the village's post mistress, Mrs. Maryanne Robertson" as one of the last to leave. The more detailed and specific sources (jf9lL9wsZ3Q, smtiBP1OPrc, FVNds4hVZ14) consistently name Helen Beatrice Taylor — her full name and birth year given. The jf9lL9wsZ3Q account is the most detailed and is likely correct.

## Emily Taylor — The Mother Who Lost Everything

- Lived in the Laundry Cottages
- The Taylor family ran the village laundry from these cottages — all washing for Tyneham House and the Rectory arrived "in wicker hampers by cart every Monday morning"
- Lost all **three sons — William, Arthur, and Bert — in World War One**
- Died in 1917, aged 52
- **"Simply worn out with work, worry and grief for her lost children."** (EvX1Kd0homg, from an information board)
- One transcript adds: *"Father was a solitary man. There was nothing he'd like better than to go out in the woods rather than having to endure the endless banter and babbling of human beings."*
- Her daughter Helen took over the laundry in 1917 and remained until the 1943 evacuation

## The Gould Family — The Gardeners

- **Tom Gould**: head gardener at Tyneham House for **40 years**; served in World War One, returned and took up the position in 1925. Wife: **Virtue**. Son: **John**. John's wife: **Murial**.
- John Gould was **fighting in World War Two** when the eviction notice arrived at Tyneham. He returned from active service to find he had no home to go back to.
- John went on to **write to Prime Minister Harold Wilson** protesting the broken promise; he was **invited to 10 Downing Street — "all to no avail"** (smtiBP1OPrc)
- In **1975**, John Gould (by then an old man) led a symbolic protest — he walked past the military barriers into the village and declared to journalists: **"I am coming home."** It was futile: the home he returned to no longer existed. (2I5XZzzvovQ)
- The church display reads: "Jim Curtis [also Thomas Gould] wants to come home. 1943 committee — John Gould was born at Tyneham 60 years ago. He wants to come home." (EvX1Kd0homg — likely paraphrased from a protest sign)

## Philip Draper — Chairman, Tyneham Action Group

One of the original Tyneham residents; became the most visible spokesperson for the campaign to return.

**Direct quotes from his interview (m-smU0jdJzY, filmed ~25–30 years after the 1943 evacuation):**

> **On what he lost:** "I always used to think that I had the nicest cottage anywhere in this area and that this area was the best place in England and that England in those days was the best country in the world. So that's what I think I lost."

> **On compensation:** "We were told that we would be bought out by compulsory purchase and naturally the price we got for it was a very low pre-war level."

> **On the campaign's goal:** "We want to see modern training for the army on the other side of the range of hills and this valley released from being an overshoot area so that it can go back to proper farming, fishing and with footpath access for the public."

> **On the value of the valley:** "Apart from being the most beautiful part of the south coast, in my opinion, it has other very important attractions. Geologically, it is of prime importance — it's a textbook case, it's equivalent to about 2,000 feet depth of Earth all tipped up on edge. Furthermore, for archaeologists, there's the Iron Age earthwork encampment on the top of the hill which is the only coastal hill fort in England and is as good, I think, as Maiden Castle at Dorchester which is so famous."

> **On the army:** "We have nothing against the army personnel — we are patriotic people — but we think the Ministry of Defence could well ensure that our valley here is returned for peaceful uses."

> **On electronic alternatives:** "There are various means these days, modern means, whereby live shells wouldn't have to be fired — I mean by that electronic simulators — which could save firing live shells which at present are costing about £4 million a year."

> **On whether original residents would actually return:** "Most of the villagers were middle-aged people when they were turned out, and now 25 years later it's doubtful if they would want to uproot themselves again. But on the other hand, there are farmers absolutely itching to get back onto this good farming land."

Philip Draper was chairman of the group that picketed visitors to Tyneham during the open days "with protest notices and photographs of the village as it was 30 years ago" (m-smU0jdJzY).

## The Driscoll Family — The Post Office

- Sergeant William Driscoll and Miss Gwendoline Driscoll, 1938–1943
- Gwendoline was the **last postmistress** of Tyneham Post Office
- Earlier postmistress: **Jane Pitman** (1907)
- **Joan Driscoll** delivered the mail at age 16 (smtiBP1OPrc)
- In 1982 two people (Peter and Colin — presumably relatives) came back to see the old post office garden (EvX1Kd0homg)
- The information board notes: "Widow Moss was head of household... daughter Mary Amelia, 30, teacher's assistant; sons William James, 28 (estate mason); son Arthur, 25; son Henry Davis, 24." (7RIxF46D8FU — from the 1918 census)

## The Whitlock Family — The Labourer's Cottage

- Ernest and Evelyn Whitlock, children Ronald and Ellen
- Lived in No. 2 The Row (Labourer's Cottage) 1933–42/43
- Ernest worked at Tyneham Farm
- When troops were billeted at Tyneham during the war, Ernest became the **unofficial barber** for the soldiers (smtiBP1OPrc)
- The cottage has a small bread oven attached to the fireplace

## The Last Survivor

- The last surviving **original resident of Tyneham** died in **May 2025**, aged 100
- In **2024**, he made one final trip back to see the house where he was born
- **"The story of Tyneham almost well and truly came to an end when just last month in May of 2025, the last surviving resident died at the age of 100."** (Ldl_u6MY_fg — the presenter dates this video to 2025)

## Oral History — Village Life

**From the WAAF who was stationed at Tyneham House (byKvdDDwMpQ — a recorded reminiscence):**
> "This is reminiscing and thinking back about 66 years ago, my time in the west as a 19-year-old radar operator, living in what was the old rectory... There was no [Bond] family living there then, so the girls were billeted in this old empty rectory — we were just in rooms with armed bedsteads, no other furniture, and the men were camping on the canvas in the village. When more people arrived, they decided the girls should be billeted in what was the old manor house — a beautiful old house that had been in the Bond family, a well-known Dorset family that had lived in this house for many many generations."

**Historian Linda Price (local historian who spoke to many former residents — VVNrWYK8fm8):**
> "They all got a letter... they had basically a month to get out... they expected to come back, you know — that's what's very poignant about it. They thought the war would end, they would come back, and all would be as it was."

> "When I look at Tyneham, what I think about is how much dislocation, how much destruction, what a human cost there is even to winning a war, even to non-combatants. The level of self-sacrifice that was necessary to conduct the kind of total war that was required to defeat the Nazis — that kind of effort boggles the modern mind."

**Worbarrow Bay fishermen (VBSNblVxV9U — the very short "Lauren learns history" clip):**
> "Sitting here, I can just imagine all the fishermen bustling about on their boats early in the morning. In the 1930s, as more and more people started to own cars, this stunning little spot became popular with tourists and the fishermen, seeing an opportunity, would sell cooked crabs and lobsters, homemade beers and cream teas."
