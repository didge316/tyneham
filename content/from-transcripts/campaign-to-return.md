# Campaign to Return

Sources: 2I5XZzzvovQ, jf9lL9wsZ3Q, m-smU0jdJzY, smtiBP1OPrc, FVNds4hVZ14, EvX1Kd0homg, Zsdo_mHBnNo, ZvdwhpTAC4k

---

## Overview

After the compulsory purchase order of 1948, the displaced residents of Tyneham — and later a wider public movement — campaigned for decades to get the land back or at least open it to public access. They never succeeded in returning, but they did force a partial compromise in the late 1970s.

---

## Timeline of the campaign

**1948:** Compulsory purchase order issued. The village is now legally owned by the Ministry of Defence. Residents are definitively told they cannot return.

**1950s–1970s:** Numerous legal challenges, demonstrations, and "even attacks on the Range infrastructure" (jf9lL9wsZ3Q). The campaign was waged primarily through letters to MPs, to *The Times*, and to the War Office.

**Late 1960s (c. 1968):** A dwindling campaign was "brought to life by protesters advocating for positive action" (EvX1Kd0homg). Protests at Tyneham drew attention to the broken promise.

**1973:** "The New Jet Report recommended the release of all 7,200 acres of army land at Lulworth — campaigners were jubilant, but the area's economy might suffer and many locals were furious." A counter-movement formed: the "Keep the Army In" committee. (EvX1Kd0homg — this passage is from a display board in the church.) There were thus two sides: those who wanted the land back, and locals economically dependent on the military presence.

**1975:** One of the original villagers, **John Gould**, "by then an old man, led a symbolic protest — he walked past the military barriers, entered the village, and declared to the few journalists who followed: 'I am coming home.'" (2I5XZzzvovQ) It was a moving act of defiance, but futile. The home he returned to no longer existed.

**Mid-1970s:** Public mood began to shift. Growing interest in local history and pressure on the military to open its vast land holdings forced a change.

**Late 1970s (c. 1977–78):** The Army proposed to demolish the dilapidated village buildings. This caused an enormous public protest. The result was a compromise: "as far as possible, the village would be preserved and open to the public and former residents when the tank ranges were not live." (jf9lL9wsZ3Q)

The ranges were eventually opened for 137 days per year, with £100,000 to be spent on increasing public access (ZvdwhpTAC4k — from a display board).

## Key figures

**Philip Draper** — chairman of the Tyneham Action Group, one of the original residents. Interviewed (m-smU0jdJzY) approximately 25–30 years after the evacuation:

> **On what he lost:** "I always used to think that I had the nicest cottage anywhere in this area and that this area was the best place in England and that England in those days was the best country in the world. So that's what I think I lost."

> **On compensation:** "We were told that we would be bought out by compulsory purchase and naturally the price we got for it was a very low pre-war level."

> **On the campaign's goal:** "We want to see modern training for the army on the other side of the range of hills and this valley released from being an overshoot area so that it can go back to proper farming, fishing and with footpath access for the public."

> **On why Tyneham is worth fighting for:** "Apart from being the most beautiful part of the south coast in my opinion, it has other very important attractions. Geologically, it is of prime importance — it's a textbook case, it's equivalent to about 2,000 feet depth of Earth all tipped up on edge. Furthermore, for archaeologists, there's the Iron Age earthwork encampment on the top of the hill which is the only coastal hill fort in England and is as good, I think, as Maiden Castle at Dorchester which is so famous."

> **On the army specifically:** "We have nothing against the army personnel — we are patriotic people — but we think the Ministry of Defence could well ensure that our valley here is returned for peaceful uses. We don't feel that it is essential to the army tank ranges on the other side of the hill."

> **On electronic alternatives to live firing:** "There are various means these days, modern means, whereby live shells wouldn't have to be fired — I mean by that electronic simulators — which could save firing live shells which at present are costing about £4 million a year."

> **On whether original residents would actually return:** "Most of the villagers were middle-aged people when they were turned out, and now 25 years later it's doubtful if they would want to uproot themselves again. But on the other hand, there are farmers absolutely itching to get back onto this good farming land."

**John Gould** — born at Tyneham, gardener's cottage family; his father Tom Gould was head gardener at Tyneham House for 40 years. John was fighting in WWII when the eviction happened. He went on to write to Prime Minister Harold Wilson and was invited to No. 10 Downing Street — "all to no avail" (smtiBP1OPrc). The church display shows: "Jim Curtis [also described as Tom Gould] wants to come home. 1943 committee John Gould was born at Tyneham 60 years ago. He wants to come home." (EvX1Kd0homg)

**Ralph Bond** — last owner of Tyneham House, compensated for the compulsory purchase but "died in 1952 still bitter over the government's handling of the evacuation and broken promises" (U0mULNSpG8Q).

## The outcome

The village was never returned. The compromise of the late 1970s gave the public restricted access on weekends, bank holidays and school holidays. The church and school were preserved and turned into museums. Most of the cottages were left to ruin, though the walls were strengthened for safety.

The last original resident died in May 2025 at age 100. He made a final visit in 2024.

**Quotable (2I5XZzzvovQ):** "Most of Tyneham's original residents went to their graves in exile, never setting foot in their homes again."

**Quotable (Zsdo_mHBnNo, display board):** "They call this the village that died for D-Day. I think that's quite true... we still need to train our soldiers and they need to be effective. So let's enjoy the peace and quiet of Tyneham at the times when they're not firing, and remember why it's here and why it's been abandoned."

## Two sides of the argument (for nuanced content)

From a church display (EvX1Kd0homg): "Whether you watch with the army's continuing presence or not, Tyneham valley is escaping the unsightly tourist development only too prominent along the adjacent coastline, untouched by modern intensive farming practices — even for wildlife..."

This is the paradox: the military's refusal to leave has accidentally preserved one of the most ecologically rich and visually unspoiled valleys on the Dorset coast. Many conservationists now regard the MOD's occupation as the reason the area has survived intact.
