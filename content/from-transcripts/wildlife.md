## The Military-as-Protector Paradox

This is the strongest wildlife theme across the transcripts and the most useful for web content.

**From a church display board (EvX1Kd0homg):**
> "Whether you watch with the army's continuing presence or not, Tyneham valley is escaping the unsightly tourist development only too prominent along the adjacent coastline — untouched by modern intensive farming practices, even for wildlife."

**From the campaign compromise display (ZvdwhpTAC4k):**
> "The village that died for D-Day... we still need to train our soldiers and they need to be effective. So let's enjoy the peace and quiet of Tyneham at the times when they're not firing, and remember why it's here and why it's been abandoned."

**Implication across multiple transcripts**: 80+ years of military exclusion has kept out the development, intensive agriculture, and tourist infrastructure that have transformed the rest of the Purbeck coast. The result is one of the most ecologically intact valleys in southern England. The school nature study books left open on the desks are said to record the same wildlife "still seen in and around Tyneham" today as in the pre-war era (EvX1Kd0homg, from the school exhibition).

## Marine Life

- **Shore crabs**: found in rock pools at Kimmeridge Bay during low tide. "I love being out to get down on my hands and knees and explore this prehistoric world" (bp8gutJXm0Y). "The shore crab is the most common crab found on the UK coast — they feast on anything they can find from seaweed, mussels and barnacles to sometimes even smaller crabs."
- **Ammonite fossils**: found in rock slabs at Kimmeridge — "the remains of an ammonite just lying within this giant slab of rock" (bp8gutJXm0Y)
- **Periwinkles**: noted in the rock pools at Kimmeridge

**Kimmeridge Bay** is described as "famed for its fossils" and is home to the Etches Collection (a museum of fossils collected by Steve Etches, a local fossil collector born 1949) (bp8gutJXm0Y).

## Geology and Palaeontology

The transcripts are much richer on geology than ecology:

- **Purbeck Monocline** (Worbarrow Bay): "The rocks at Worbarrow Bay that are all tilted are actually part of something called the Purbeck Monocline — a gigantic geological fold related to the formation of the Alps. The African plate moved north and collided with the Eurasian Plate, buckling and folding rocks, forming the Alps, and the tectonic forces rippled out folding rocks we see along the Jurassic Coast today." (qxakRTadU-M, Jurassic Coast Trust)
- **Dinosaur footprints**: "Geologists have actually found evidence of dinosaur footprints and trackways in the rocks at Worbarrow Bay." (qxakRTadU-M)
- **Jurassic Coast UNESCO World Heritage Site**: "a staggering 95-mile stretch of coastline... a ribbon of cliffs and coves that holds 185 million years of Earth's history. Fossils of ancient sea dragons are pulled from its shores." (2I5XZzzvovQ)
- **Kimmeridge shale**: used by Romans to distil seawater and collect salt; the area also has an oil field producing since the early 1960s (bp8gutJXm0Y)
- **Fossilised Forest** (between Kimmeridge and Lulworth Cove): ancient cypress-like trees pickled by salt in a warm lagoon, slowly mineralised — "pickled wood." The algae mats that formed around the trees are what remains, the fossilised wood having been removed by Victorians. Fossilised salt crystals also visible. (UJhiuu6WjWQ)

## The School Nature Study Connection

The village school at Tyneham placed a strong emphasis on nature study:
- "The schoolroom display highlights the wealth of wildlife that can still be seen in and around Tyneham — much of it familiar to pupils of the pre-war era" (EvX1Kd0homg, school exhibition text)
- Nature study books are left open on the desks
- The 1856 school included "nature walks in the surrounding vales to identify flora and fauna" (2I5XZzzvovQ)
- Books visible include: "different types of shellfish that gathered", "different types of sheep", children's encyclopaedia entries on natural history

This creates a useful content angle: the same wildlife that the Tyneham children learned about in their nature lessons is still present in the valley today.
