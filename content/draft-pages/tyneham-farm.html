Tyneham Farm

By James Langton · Updated June 2026

Tyneham Farm stands about 300 metres south of the village centre, down the lane past the church and through the old gateway. Of the original farm complex — dairyman's house, farmhouses, barns, stables, and outbuildings — only the stable block, yard, and granary survive intact. The Great Barn remains the most substantial agricultural structure in the village and is open to visitors.

The farm was the economic engine of the Tyneham estate for at least 80 years. It is also, unexpectedly, the building in the village that most makes visitors feel they have stepped into a moment that was never resolved.

The Smith Family and the Working Farm

The Smith family ran Tyneham Farm from around 1860. By the Edwardian period, Walter K. Smith and his wife Kate were running the operation — a photograph from 1929 shows Walter in his Austin tourer on the farm lane. The Smiths stayed until 1940, when Sydney Churchill — no relation to Winston — took over and kept the farm going through the first years of the war.

By 1943, the farm was a working operation with cattle, horses, and some of the earliest tractor machinery in the valley. When the Army requisitioned Tyneham in December of that year, the farm closed along with everything else. Sydney Churchill had to leave. The livestock went with the farmers; some of the machinery did not.

The Great Barn: Village Theatre, 1899–1914

Before it was a working barn in the modern sense, the Great Barn had a different life entirely. From around 1899 to 1914, the Bond family opened the barn as the venue for the village's theatrical productions — plays and pantomimes that drew audiences from across the surrounding area. People came by horse-drawn carriage from Wareham and the villages of Purbeck to fill the barn for an evening's entertainment.

The First World War ended the theatre. By the 1920s and 1930s the barn had returned to agricultural use. The theatrical history was largely forgotten until local historians documented it in the latter half of the 20th century. In 2009, the barn was reinstated as an exhibition and event space — the same building, the same function returned after nearly a century.

What's Inside the Barn Today

The barn now houses the main agricultural exhibition for Tyneham, with a collection of farm equipment that spans the estate's working life. Some pieces were brought in by local historians over the past 15 to 20 years; much of it is original to the farm.

The centrepiece is a Fordson Major tractor from the early 1940s — rusted and long since seized, it sits where it was presumably left in 1943. The most likely reason it wasn't taken: it was already broken down and too difficult to remove, and probably formed part of a subsequent compensation claim against the War Office. Cyril Barnes, another farmer in the valley, had brought one of the first tractors into the area; he had to leave his behind as well.

Alongside the tractor sits a carefully restored hay wagon — an original piece kept in working condition. The stables along the south wall still have their six horse stalls, though the original timber is deteriorating. The granary and seed barn are also accessible, as is the carriage shed.

"I have to say I found walking through the barns quite eerie! Unlike maybe the church and the school — walking through the farm really does make it feel like everybody just upped and left in 1943."

Visitor, quoted in documentary footage

What Has Been Lost

The Dairyman's House — which once stood adjacent to the main barn — is gone. So are most of the original farmhouses. The MOD demolished several of the more deteriorated structures in the decades after the war, a decision that has since attracted criticism from those who believe the buildings could have been stabilised. The hen house and bull house (which once housed what local records describe as a "Monster Devon Bull") no longer stand.

The stable block's original timbers are, according to those who manage the site, "literally rotting away" — a reminder that conservation at Tyneham is an ongoing challenge, not a solved problem. The MOD, which manages the site as part of the Lulworth Ranges, carries out maintenance work but has limited resources for full restoration.

Visiting the Farm

The farm buildings are open on the same schedule as the rest of Tyneham — weekends and school holidays when the military ranges are not in use. From the car park, follow the lane past St Mary's Church and continue south for about five minutes. The Great Barn is clearly signposted and unmissable.

The farm is roughly 300 metres from the main exhibition area in the church and school, so most visitors combine both in a single visit. There is no entrance fee for any part of Tyneham — the car park operates on an honesty-box basis.
