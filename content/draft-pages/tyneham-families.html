The Families of Tyneham

By James Langton · Updated June 2026

The Bond family owned Tyneham for 260 years, but they were never more than a small part of its life. The village was held together by the families who worked the land, ran the laundry, delivered the post, taught the children, and tended the gardens — people who had often lived there for generations and who, on 19 December 1943, were given 28 days to leave and never came back.

Their names are recorded on the information boards that line the walls of St Mary's Church and the roofless cottages of The Row. This is their story.

The Taylor Family — The Laundry Cottages

The Taylors lived in the Laundry Cottages on the east side of the village — the only property in Tyneham with running water, fed by a spring. Their work was the village laundry: all the washing for Tyneham House and the Rectory arrived in wicker hampers by cart every Monday morning.

Emily Taylor was the matriarch of the cottage. The First World War took all three of her sons — William, Arthur, and Bert — and she did not survive them long. She died in 1917, aged 52, described on an information board as having been "simply worn out with work, worry and grief for her lost children."

Her daughter Helen took over the laundry and stayed on at the cottages for the next 26 years. It was Helen Beatrice Taylor — born in 1901, aged 44 at the evacuation — who pinned the handwritten note to the church door on the last day, 19 December 1943: "Please treat the church and houses with care. We have given up our homes where many of us have lived for generations, to help win the war to keep men free. We will return one day and thank you for treating the village kindly."

Helen never did return to live there. She died in 1998 — 55 years after the evacuation, having spent more than half a century away from the village where she was born.

The Gould Family — The Gardener's Cottage

Tom Gould served in the First World War, came home, and spent the next 40 years as head gardener at Tyneham House. He and his wife Virtue raised their son John in the Gardener's Cottage. The Gardener's Cottage still stands at the edge of the village, fenced off and no longer safe to enter.

John Gould was away fighting in the Second World War when the eviction notice arrived at Tyneham. He returned from active service to find he no longer had a home to go back to. He spent years trying to reverse the decision. He wrote to Prime Minister Harold Wilson protesting the broken promise and was invited to 10 Downing Street — to no avail.

In 1975, John Gould led a symbolic protest. Now an old man, he walked past the military barriers into the village and declared to the journalists waiting there: "I am coming home." The house he returned to no longer existed.

Philip Draper — Voice of the Campaign

Philip Draper was among the original residents of Tyneham and became the most visible spokesperson for the campaign to return. As chairman of the group that picketed visitors to Tyneham during open days — carrying protest notices and photographs of the village as it had been — he gave filmed interviews in which he articulated what had been taken.

"I always used to think that I had the nicest cottage anywhere in this area and that this area was the best place in England and that England in those days was the best country in the world. So that's what I think I lost."

His argument for returning the valley went beyond sentiment. He made a detailed case for its scientific value: "Geologically, it is of prime importance — it's a textbook case, it's equivalent to about 2,000 feet depth of Earth all tipped up on edge. Furthermore, for archaeologists, there's the Iron Age earthwork encampment on the top of the hill which is the only coastal hill fort in England and is as good, I think, as Maiden Castle at Dorchester."

He was also pragmatic about the residents themselves. Interviewed roughly 25 years after the evacuation, he acknowledged that most original villagers were now too old to uproot again, but added: "There are farmers absolutely itching to get back onto this good farming land." The argument was never won.

The Driscoll Family — The Post Office

Sergeant William Driscoll and his sister Gwendoline lived in No. 3 The Row from 1938 to 1943. Gwendoline was the last postmistress of Tyneham Post Office. Her niece Joan Driscoll had delivered the village mail at the age of 16. The post office also served as a general store: bags of flour on the floor, and before 1929, the only telephone in the village was in its back room — before that, villagers relied on telegrams entirely.

The Whitlock Family — The Labourer's Cottage

Ernest and Evelyn Whitlock lived in No. 2 The Row from 1933 to 1943 with their children Ronald and Ellen. Ernest worked at Tyneham Farm. When soldiers were billeted in the village during the war, he became their unofficial barber — a practical man finding a practical use for a difficult situation. The cottage still has a small bread oven set into the fireplace.

Other Families

The information boards in the church record dozens of families across the village's last century. The Upshore family occupied the Shepherd's Cottage from 1925 to 1929 — a photograph shows Shepherd Bill Upshore with his dog Sam. The Wellman family lived in Gwyle Cottages from 1912 to 1936; the Grant family followed until 1942. Arthur Grant visited decades later and spotted himself as a child in a 1927 photograph on one of the information boards.

At Tyneham Farm, the Smith family ran the operation from 1860 to 1940. Walter K. Smith was photographed in his Austin tourer in 1929. Sydney Churchill — no relation to Winston — took the farm over in 1940 and ran it until the Army closed it in 1943. Cyril Barnes had brought one of the first tractors into the valley; he had to leave it behind at the evacuation.

Inside the church, a memorial plaque records Hannah Herworth of County Durham, who died in 1933 aged 86 after "45 years devoted service" in the Bond household — a quiet measure of the lives that revolved around the estate.

The Last Survivor

The last surviving original resident of Tyneham died in May 2025, aged 100. In 2024, he had made one final trip back to see the house where he was born. With his death, the living memory of Tyneham before the evacuation passed into history.

What Visitors to Tyneham Say

Historian Linda Price, who interviewed many of the former residents, reflected on what the evacuation meant in human terms:

"They all got a letter. They had basically a month to get out. They expected to come back — that's what's very poignant about it. They thought the war would end, they would come back, and all would be as it was."

"When I look at Tyneham, what I think about is how much dislocation, how much destruction, what a human cost there is even to winning a war, even to non-combatants. The level of self-sacrifice that was necessary to conduct the kind of total war that was required to defeat the Nazis — that kind of effort boggles the modern mind."
