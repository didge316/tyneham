Smuggling at Tyneham

Long before Tyneham became known as the village that time forgot, its coastline had a very different reputation. For much of the 18th and early 19th centuries, the remote coves and clifftop paths around the Tyneham valley were among the most active smuggling corridors on the entire south coast of England.

The geography was almost perfectly suited to the trade. Worbarrow Bay offered a wide shingle beach with deep enough water for large vessels to approach close inshore. The cliffs on either side provided cover and vantage points, while the maze of inland tracks through the Purbeck hills gave loaded packhorses a dozen ways to disappear before dawn. The nearest town of any size was miles away, and the local population was small enough that everyone either knew what was happening or had reason to stay quiet.

Worbarrow Bay: A Smugglers' Highway

By the early 18th century, smuggling had grown from a local cottage industry into something far more organised. Runs were planned weeks in advance, ships were hired or purpose-built, and the networks of buyers, carriers and lookouts stretched from the Dorset coast all the way to London.

In October 1719, Worbarrow Bay was the scene of one of the largest landings ever recorded on the Purbeck coast. Five ships unloaded simultaneously on the beach — an operation so brazen that a witness compared the scene to a market day. Armed men controlled the shore while horses and wagons carried the cargo inland before the customs officers could muster any meaningful response. It was, by any measure, an operation conducted in open defiance of the law.

Arish Mell, the smaller beach a short distance to the west and technically within the same stretch of coastline, was used in similar fashion. The shingle there shelved steeply enough for boats to unload directly onto the beach, and the narrow valley behind offered quick access to the inland tracks.

By 1804 the scale of the trade had become impossible for the authorities to ignore. A report from the Weymouth Custom House that year named Arish Mell and Worbarrow Beach among the most active landing sites on this section of coast, estimating that tens of thousands of casks of contraband were being brought ashore annually. Large vessels carrying hundreds of casks at a time would stand offshore and unload, and the report noted bluntly that the officers stationed in the area were simply not sufficient in number to stop them.

What Was Being Smuggled?

Brandy was the most common cargo — cheap to source in France, valuable in England, and easy to move in casks. Tea was another staple of the trade, heavily taxed and in high demand among all classes. Gin, tobacco and fine French lace completed the typical inventory of a Purbeck run.

Not all of what was landed stayed in Dorset. There is evidence that some contraband made its way to London markets, carried by night along established routes through the inland counties. Locally, the farmers and landowners of the Purbeck hills were known to be among the best customers — just as likely to be waiting with coin and a cellar as they were to be reporting anything to the authorities.

Violence on the Cliffs

For most of the 18th century, the relationship between smugglers and customs men was one of wary avoidance rather than outright conflict. Outnumbered and underpaid, revenue officers generally knew better than to confront a large and well-armed landing party. But by the 1820s and 1830s, the mood was changing. The government had reorganised the coastguard service, stationed men more systematically along the cliffs, and made clear it expected results.

The response from those who stood to lose the most was brutal. In the early 1830s, there were several attacks on coastguard men along this stretch of coast. Two were thrown from the cliffs to their deaths — one of them at Gad Cliff, the dramatic headland that forms the western wall of Worbarrow Bay and falls within the parish of Tyneham. It was a line that had been crossed, and the authorities responded with greater numbers and harsher sentences.

The coastguard station established at Worbarrow was a direct consequence of this intensification. By the mid-19th century, a small community of coastguard families lived in the cottages at the foot of the valley, and their presence gradually suppressed the trade that had thrived there for so long.

Local Men Who Paid the Price

As enforcement tightened, so did the courts' appetite for convictions. Parish records and gaol registers from the 1820s and 1840s reveal the names of ordinary Tyneham and Worbarrow men who were caught and punished — fishermen and labourers who had grown up in a place where smuggling was simply part of how things worked.

Name Occupation Year Age Sentence
George Mowlam Fisherman, Worbarrow 1821 40 £100 fine, 1 year
James Galton Labourer, Egliston 1822 53 £100 fine, 1 year
William Stickland Fisherman, Tyneham 1840 26 6 months
Thomas Stickland Fisherman, Tyneham 1840 23 6 months

The Stickland family were one of the oldest in Tyneham, their name appearing in the parish records for generations. William and Thomas — likely brothers — were both fishermen in their twenties when they were convicted in the same year, 1840. A £100 fine, handed to George Mowlam in 1821, would have been a devastating sum for a working fisherman at the time — the equivalent of many months' wages.

These were not gang leaders or wealthy merchants; they were local men doing what their fathers and grandfathers had done, in a place where the sea offered limited ways to make a living. By the 1840s, however, the era of large-scale coastal smuggling in Purbeck was largely over. Improved coastguard coverage, lower import duties and more effective policing had combined to make the trade far riskier and far less profitable than it had once been.

A History Written in the Landscape

When you walk down to Worbarrow Bay today, past the empty coastguard cottages and out onto the open shingle, it is easy to see why this spot was so attractive to those who worked by night. The bay is still remote, still quiet, still cut off from the rest of the world by a ring of hills and an army firing range. The cliffs of Gad Cliff rise to the west just as they did when contraband was being carried across them in the dark.

The smuggling era left no monuments and few traces. But it is woven into the history of this coast as surely as the chalk cliffs themselves — a reminder that the people who lived here before the evacuation of 1943 had always found ways to survive on their own terms.
